Review

\[ E[\text{birth weight}] = 3100g \] \[ V[\text{birth weight}] = 400^2 \]

Review

From the mathematical model, we can calculate population quantities like the range of the middle 95% of the distribution.

\[ \text{IQR} = 3370 \text{ to } 2830\]

NEW

We are also interested in transformations of samples of size \(N\).

\[\text{sample mean: }\ g(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N X_i \]

NEW

We are also interested in transformations of samples of size \(N\).

\[\begin{align*}\text{sample variance: }&\\\ g(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N) &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N (X_i - \bar{X})^2 \\ &=\left[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^NX_i^2\right] - [\bar{X}]^2 \end{align*}\]

NEW

We are also interested in transformations of samples of size \(N\).

\[\begin{align*}&\text{median: }\ g(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N) = \\ &\text{smallest }\ x\ \text{ such that}\ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i = 1}^N I(X_i \leq x) = .5\end{align*}\]

NEW

We are also interested in transformations of samples of size \(N\).

\[\begin{align*}&\text{min}(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N)\\ &\text{max}(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N)\end{align*}\]

NEW

The distribution of outcomes derived from samples (min, max, median, mean, variance) is called the sampling distribution.

Just like the variance for BMI is related to the spread of BMI values when sampling individuals, the variance of the sampling distribution of summary statistics (mean, variance, median, min) is related to the spread of the summary measures when sampling studies of the same size.

When we get to inference

The width of the sampling distribution of the mean is related to the precision with which the mean BMI is estimated.

One way to measure the width of the distribution is to calculate the spread of the middle 95% of the distribution.

Example

hist(
    mean_bmi
  , breaks = 100
  , main = "Samping distribution of BMI"
  , freq = FALSE
)
m95 <- quantile(mean_bmi, c(0.025, 0.975))
abline(v = m95, col = "blue", lty = 2)

A tigher sampling distribution translates to more precise estimates of the mean.

Example

mean_bmi <- array(NA, dim = c(5000,2))
for(i in 1:nrow(mean_bmi)){
  mean_bmi[i, 1] <- rnorm(200, 25, 6.5) %>% mean
  mean_bmi[i, 2] <- rnorm(400, 25, 6.5) %>% mean
}

par(mfrow = c(2,1), mar = c(4,2,1,1))
for(j in 1:2){
  hist(
      mean_bmi[,j]
    , breaks = 100
    , main = c("N = 200","N = 400")[j]
    , xlab = ""
    , freq = FALSE
    , xlim = range(mean_bmi[,1])
  )
  m95 <- mean_bmi[,j] %>% quantile(c(0.025, 0.975))
  abline(v = m95, col = "blue", lty = 2)
}

Analytic tools for quantiles

You can simulate the sampling distribution of quantiles. Now, lets discuss analytic tools for understanding sample quantiles.

ECDF

The empirical cumulative density function

\[\text{Let } \boldsymbol{X}_N = X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N\]

\[ \text{ecdf}(x, \boldsymbol{X}_N) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^NI(X_i \leq x) \]

ECDF in R

bmi <- rnorm(25, 25, 6.5) # New sample of size 25
bmi_ecdf <- ecdf(bmi)  # The output is a function
bmi_ecdf(30)
## [1] 0.84

ECDF in R

plot(bmi_ecdf, do.points = FALSE)

ECDF in R

Note the connection with the CDF.

Order statistics

\[\text{If } \boldsymbol{X}_N = X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_N\] \[\text{Let } \boldsymbol{X}_{(N)} = \text{sort}(\boldsymbol{X}_N)\]

\[\boldsymbol{X}_{(N)} = X_{(1)}, X_{(2)}, \ldots, X_{(N)}\]

Order statistics

Order statistic
\(1^{st}\) \(X_{(1)}\) min
\(2^{nd}\) \(X_{(2)}\) \(2^{nd}\) smallest value
\(\ldots\)
\(N^{th}\) \(X_{(N)}\) max

What is the distribution of \(X_{(k)}\)?

Order statistics

What is the distribution of \(X_{(k)}\)?

\[ f_{(k)}(x) = k\, {n \choose k}[F(x)]^{k-1}[1-F(x)]^{n-k}f(x)\]

Order statistics

What is the distribution of \(X_{(k)}\)?

fk <- function(x,k,n){
  k*
  choose(n,k)*
  (pnorm(x, 25, 6.5))^(k-1)*
  (1-pnorm(x, 25, 6.5))^(n-k)*
  dnorm(x, 25, 6.5)
}

What is the distribution of \(X_{(k)}\)?

\[ F_{(k)}(x) = \sum_{j = k}^N {n \choose j}[F(x)]^{j}[1-F(x)]^{n-j}\]

Order statistics

What is the distribution of \(X_{(k)}\)?

Fk <- function(x,k,n){
  pbinom(k-1, n, pnorm(x, 25, 6.5), lower.tail = FALSE)
}
